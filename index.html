Laboratory Exercise No. 4: Plant Embryogenesis & Seed Development

GBIO 100 – Developmental Biology Laboratory

Mode: Asynchronous
Central Concept: From zygote to mature embryo
Overall Goal: Analyze plant embryo development

Laboratory Overview

This lab traces the developmental journey from a single-celled zygote to a mature, dormant embryo within a seed, highlighting the establishment of the plant body plan and the differences between monocots and dicots.

Through this asynchronous laboratory experience, you will analyze provided micrographs, diagrams, and datasets to understand the stages of plant embryo development, the structure of seeds, and the biological significance of this process.

Pre-Lab Quiz

Complete this quiz to test your understanding of the pre-lab materials before proceeding to the virtual investigation. After submitting, you'll see the correct answers with explanations.

1. Which of the following is the correct sequence of plant embryo development?

Correct Answer: Zygote → Globular → Heart → Torpedo → Mature

Explanation: Plant embryo development begins with the zygote, which undergoes cell division to form a globular stage. This then develops into the heart stage as cotyledons begin to form, followed by the torpedo stage where the embryo elongates, and finally the mature embryo stage.

2. What is the primary function of cotyledons in a seed?

Correct Answer: Storage of nutrients for the embryo

Explanation: Cotyledons, also known as seed leaves, serve as nutrient storage organs in seeds. They provide the necessary energy and building blocks for the embryo during germination until the seedling can photosynthesize on its own.

3. Which structure protects the shoot apex in monocot embryos?

Correct Answer: Coleoptile

Explanation: The coleoptile is a protective sheath that covers the plumule (shoot apex) in monocot embryos. It helps the shoot push through the soil during germination while protecting the delicate shoot apex.

4. What is the main difference between albuminous and exalbuminous seeds?

Correct Answer: The presence or absence of endosperm at maturity

Explanation: Albuminous seeds retain endosperm as a food storage tissue at maturity (e.g., corn, wheat), while exalbuminous seeds have no endosperm at maturity because it's completely consumed during embryo development (e.g., bean, pea).

5. Which plant hormone plays a crucial role in breaking seed dormancy?

Correct Answer: Gibberellin

Explanation: Gibberellins are plant hormones that stimulate seed germination by promoting the synthesis of hydrolytic enzymes that break down stored food reserves in the seed, enabling the embryo to grow.

Post-Lab Report Guidelines

IMRaD Format Requirements

Your formal laboratory report must follow the IMRaD (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) format. Use the structured worksheet provided to organize your findings from all four stations into a coherent scientific narrative.

Introduction

Explain the purpose and importance of plant embryogenesis. Provide background information on the developmental process from zygote to mature embryo.

Methods

Summarize the "virtual" methods used for each station. Describe how you analyzed the provided micrographs, diagrams, and datasets.

Results

Present your findings from all four stations. Include descriptions, references to your analyses, and any relevant observations. Use clear headings for each station's results.

Discussion

Interpret your results and explain how embryogenesis establishes the plant body plan and ensures species survival. Connect your findings to broader biological concepts.
